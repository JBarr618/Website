Module 12I: Troubleshooting Computer Based Train Control

Troubleshooting a Computer Based Train Control (CBTC) system can be challenging due to its integrated nature and reliance on complex software, hardware, and communication networks. A systematic approach and specialized diagnostic tools are often required. Here are some common issues and troubleshooting strategies:

1. Loss of Communication

Symptom: Trains lose contact with the central control system, leading to potential service disruptions or a fallback to a more restrictive operating mode.

Troubleshooting:

  • Check the status of the radio communication network infrastructure (base stations, access points, leaky feeder).
  • Verify the onboard communication units on the affected trains.
  • Analyze signal strength and potential interference issues.
  • Examine network logs and diagnostic tools to identify communication errors or outages.

2. Train Localization Errors

Symptom: The central system or onboard systems report inaccurate train positions, potentially leading to incorrect movement authorities or ATP interventions.

Troubleshooting:

  • Check the functionality of onboard positioning sensors (odometer, balise reader).
  • Verify the integrity and calibration of balises along the track.
  • Analyze data logs from the positioning system to identify discrepancies or anomalies.
  • Calibrate or replace faulty positioning sensors.

3. Automatic Train Protection (ATP) Issues

Symptom: Unwarranted emergency brake applications or failures to intervene when safety limits are exceeded.

Troubleshooting:

  • Analyze ATP event logs to understand the circumstances of the intervention or failure.
  • Verify the accuracy of speed and position data being fed to the ATP system.
  • Check the configuration and calibration of the ATP software parameters.
  • Test the functionality of the braking system and its interface with the ATP.

4. Automatic Train Operation (ATO) Malfunctions

Symptom: Issues with automated driving functions, such as jerky movements, incorrect stopping positions, or failures to follow speed profiles.

Troubleshooting:

  • Examine ATO system logs for error codes or unusual behavior.
  • Verify the accuracy of track profile data used by the ATO.
  • Check the calibration of speed and acceleration control systems.
  • Analyze the commands being sent from the central system to the onboard ATO.

5. Central Control System Problems

Symptom: Failures in the central server, database errors, or issues with the operator workstations.

Troubleshooting:

  • Examine server logs and system health indicators.
  • Check network connectivity to all wayside and onboard units.
  • Perform database integrity checks and backups.
  • Restart or failover to redundant systems as necessary.

6. Software Glitches

Symptom: Intermittent or unpredictable system behavior, software crashes, or data corruption.

Troubleshooting:

  • Analyze software logs and error reports.
  • Restart affected software components or systems.
  • Apply software patches or updates as recommended by the vendor.
  • Revert to previous stable versions if necessary.

Troubleshooting CBTC often requires specialized diagnostic tools, access to system logs, and a thorough understanding of the system architecture and its various components. Collaboration between signaling technicians, IT specialists, and operations personnel is crucial for effective problem resolution.
