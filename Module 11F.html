🚦 Module 11F - Types of Relays Used

Common Relay Types in Signal Systems

Railway signaling systems use different types of relays depending on the function, safety requirement, electrical load, timing need, and operating environment.

Electromechanical relays are traditional relays that use a coil, magnetic field, armature, spring, and mechanical contacts to open or close circuits.

Armature relays are common electromechanical relays. When the coil is energized, the armature moves and changes the state of the relay contacts.

Reed relays use small sealed contacts inside a glass tube. They can operate quickly and are protected from dirt and moisture, but they may be more sensitive to shock or damage.

Stepping relays move through a sequence of positions each time they receive an electrical pulse. These relays can be used for memory, counting, or sequencing functions.

Latching relays are designed to hold their contact position after the coil power is removed. This makes them useful where a circuit state must be remembered without continuous coil power.

Solid-state relays use electronic semiconductor components instead of moving mechanical contacts. They can switch quickly and silently because they have no moving parts.

Safety relays are designed for critical circuits. They may use force-guided contacts so that contact failures can be detected and the circuit can move toward a safe condition.

Time delay relays are used when a circuit needs an intentional delay before picking up, dropping away, or changing state. They are useful for sequencing and timing operations.

The correct relay type must be selected for the application. In railroad signaling, safety-critical circuits normally require approved vital or fail-safe relay designs.
