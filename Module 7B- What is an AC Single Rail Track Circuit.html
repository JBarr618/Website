Module 7B: What is an AC Single Rail Track Circuit?

An AC single rail track circuit is a train detection system that uses alternating current and a unique configuration where insulated rail joints are installed on only one of the two running rails of a track to define the boundaries of a block. The other rail remains electrically continuous, often spanning multiple track circuit blocks. This continuous rail serves as a common return path for both the AC track circuit current and, in electrified railways, the AC traction power return current.

In this setup, an AC power source is connected between the insulated rail and the continuous rail at the entrance of a track block. At the exit of the same block, an AC-sensitive receiver is connected in the same manner – between the insulated rail and the continuous rail. The alternating current signal propagates from the source, travels along the insulated rail, and then returns to the receiver via the continuous, un-insulated rail.

The detection of a train relies on the principle of shunting. When a train's wheels and axles bridge the insulated rail and the continuous rail within the defined block, they create a low-impedance pathway. This low-impedance shunt diverts the AC current away from the relatively higher impedance of the receiver. The resulting drop in current or change in phase at the receiver causes it to indicate an occupied track. The "single rail" aspect highlights the key difference from double rail circuits: only one rail is segmented to form the electrically isolated block for train detection.
