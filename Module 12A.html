Computer Based Train Control Fundamentals

To understand Computer Based Train Control, it is important to understand the major parts that allow the system to safely control train movement.

One major part of CBTC is precise train location. The system must know where each train is, how fast it is moving, and what direction it is traveling.

Train location may be determined using odometers, speed sensors, balises, beacons, transponders, radar, radio systems, or other onboard and wayside equipment.

Odometers and speed sensors measure train movement by tracking wheel rotation and distance traveled. This helps the onboard system estimate the train's position along the track.

Balises or beacons provide fixed reference points along the track. When a train passes over or near one, the onboard system can confirm or correct its location.

Another major part of CBTC is continuous communication. Trains must communicate with the central control system so movement authority and safety information can be exchanged.

The train may send location, speed, direction, train status, and other operating information. The control system may send movement authority, speed profiles, and operating commands back to the train.

Centralized control and processing allow the CBTC system to evaluate train locations, braking distances, route conditions, and the position of other trains on the line.

Each CBTC-equipped train has onboard control equipment. This equipment receives movement authority and speed commands and works with Automatic Train Protection to enforce safe limits.

Movement Authority, or MA, defines how far a train is permitted to move safely. In CBTC, this authority can be updated continuously based on train speed, position, traffic, and track conditions.

Speed profiling tells the train the target speeds for different parts of its authorized movement. The onboard system monitors the train to make sure it remains within safe limits.
