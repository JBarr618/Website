Module 11B: How Relays Function

The operation of an electromechanical relay is based on the principles of electromagnetism. Here's a step-by-step breakdown of how a typical relay functions:

  1. Energizing the Coil: When current flows through the coil (a wire wrapped around an iron core), it produces a magnetic field proportional to the coil current and windings.
  2. Attracting the Armature: The magnetic field pulls the armature (a movable iron piece) toward the coil. A spring typically holds the armature in its resting state.
  3. Moving the Contacts: As the magnetic force overcomes the spring, the armature moves and causes the relay's contacts to change:
    • Normally Open (NO) contacts close and complete the circuit.
    • Normally Closed (NC) contacts open and interrupt the circuit.
    • Changeover (CO) contacts switch from NC to NO.
  4. De-energizing the Coil: When current is interrupted, the magnetic field collapses.
  5. Returning to Resting State: The spring pulls the armature back, returning all contacts to their original state:
    • NO contacts open.
    • NC contacts close.
    • CO contacts switch back to NC.

This cycle allows a low-power control circuit to safely operate a separate circuit that may carry higher voltage or current. The electrical isolation between the control and contact circuits is a critical safety advantage.
