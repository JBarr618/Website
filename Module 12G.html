Types of Computer Based Train Control Used

Computer Based Train Control systems can be designed in different ways depending on the railroad, transit agency, operating environment, and level of automation required.

Some systems are built mainly around central control equipment, while others use more onboard processing. CBTC may also support manual, semi-automatic, driverless, or fully unattended train operation.

1. Centralized CBTC Systems

Most CBTC systems in service use a centralized design. In this type of system, central or wayside control equipment receives train information, calculates safe Movement Authority, and sends commands back to the trains.

Examples of major CBTC suppliers include Siemens, Thales, and Alstom. Their systems are used in many metro and transit systems throughout the world.

2. Distributed and Hybrid CBTC Systems

Distributed CBTC systems allow more processing to take place onboard the train. This means the train may perform more calculations locally instead of depending completely on one central processor.

Some modern systems use a hybrid design. A hybrid system may combine centralized safety supervision with onboard train-based logic to improve flexibility, reliability, and system resilience.

3. Grade of Automation in CBTC

CBTC can support different Grades of Automation. The grade describes how much of the train operation is performed automatically.

GoA 2 - Semi-Automatic Operation

Many rail systems begin with GoA 2. In GoA 2, the train may start, stop, and control speed automatically, but an operator is still onboard for door operation, emergencies, and degraded operation.

GoA 3 - Driverless Train Operation

In GoA 3, the train operates without a driver in the cab. The system controls train movement and doors, but staff may still be onboard to assist passengers or respond to emergencies.

GoA 4 - Unattended Train Operation

In GoA 4, the train is fully automated with no onboard staff required for normal operation. This is common on some modern metro systems.

4. Mainline Automation Challenges

High automation is usually easier on metro systems than on mainline railroads. Metro systems often have controlled environments, shorter routes, predictable stopping patterns, and separated tracks.

Mainline railroads are more difficult to fully automate because they may have mixed traffic, longer braking distances, more complex routing, highway grade crossings, work zones, and greater obstacle detection concerns.

5. Radio Communication Technology

CBTC depends on reliable radio communication. Systems may use licensed radio, unlicensed radio, Wi-Fi-based systems, LTE, 5G, or proprietary industrial radio networks.

Reliable communication is important because trains and control systems must continuously exchange train location, speed, Movement Authority, system status, and control information.

6. Public Cellular Networks

Some newer systems may consider public cellular networks such as LTE or 5G. These systems may reduce the amount of dedicated communication infrastructure needed.

However, public cellular networks must be carefully evaluated for coverage, reliability, latency, cybersecurity, priority access, and safety-critical performance.

7. Choosing the Right CBTC Type

The type of CBTC selected depends on the railway’s needs. Important factors include service frequency, safety requirements, ridership, budget, existing signal systems, automation goals, communication coverage, and future expansion plans.
