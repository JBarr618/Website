Testing Computer Based Train Control

Testing a Computer Based Train Control system is a critical part of placing the system into service. The purpose of testing is to make sure the CBTC system is safe, reliable, properly installed, and functioning as designed.

CBTC testing happens in stages. Testing may begin in a laboratory, continue in the field after installation, and continue again during live operation through monitoring and maintenance checks.

1. Simulation and Laboratory Testing

Before CBTC equipment is placed into service on the railroad, testing is often performed in a laboratory or simulated environment.

This allows engineers to test software, onboard equipment, wayside equipment, central control equipment, interfaces, normal operation, degraded operation, and failure conditions before the system is tested on the actual track.

2. Static Testing

Static testing is performed after equipment is installed but before the train is operated dynamically under CBTC control.

Static testing may include checking wiring, power, equipment configuration, communication links, onboard computers, radios, antennas, balise readers, sensors, and driver machine interfaces.

3. Dynamic Testing

Dynamic testing is performed with trains moving on the track. This is also called on-track testing.

Dynamic testing verifies train location accuracy, wireless communication, speed supervision, braking enforcement, Movement Authority, train stopping accuracy, route interfaces, and degraded mode operation.

4. ATP Testing

Automatic Train Protection testing verifies that the system will keep the train within safe speed and distance limits.

ATP testing confirms that if the train exceeds permitted limits or approaches the end of its Movement Authority, the system will intervene and apply the brakes as required.

5. ATO Testing

If the CBTC system includes Automatic Train Operation, ATO testing is performed to verify smooth acceleration, braking, speed control, and accurate station stopping.

6. Integration Testing

Integration testing confirms that all CBTC equipment works together as one complete system.

This includes testing interfaces with interlockings, switches, signals, platform screen doors, control center equipment, communication systems, and existing railroad systems.

7. Degraded Mode Testing

Degraded mode testing verifies how the system responds when something fails or does not operate normally.

Examples include communication loss, equipment failure, sensor failure, train location errors, route changes, emergency braking, and fallback operating modes.

8. Safety Validation

Safety validation confirms that hazards have been identified, analyzed, controlled, and reduced to an acceptable level.

Formal safety methods may include Failure Mode and Effects Analysis, hazard logs, safety cases, independent safety assessments, and verification that safety requirements have been met.

9. Acceptance Testing

Acceptance testing is performed to prove that the CBTC system meets the contract requirements, design requirements, safety requirements, and operating requirements before it is accepted for service.

10. Ongoing Monitoring and Maintenance Testing

Testing does not stop after CBTC is placed into service. The system must continue to be monitored and maintained.

Ongoing testing helps detect problems early, verify communication health, confirm sensor accuracy, check software performance, and maintain safe and reliable train operation.

Review Questions

1. Why is simulation and laboratory testing important?

A. It is mainly used to train passengers before launch.

B. It allows early testing of hardware, software, interfaces, and failure scenarios before field deployment.

C. It allows field testing to be skipped.

D. It loads emergency broadcasts into station systems.

2. What is evaluated during dynamic CBTC testing?

A. Track paint wear and advertisement placement.

B. Number of passengers on board.

C. Train location, communication, ATP, ATO, degraded modes, and system interfaces.

D. Employee uniform fit and horn sound level.

3. What is the purpose of safety validation?

A. To make sure trains have correct branding decals.

B. To test passenger Wi-Fi at stations.

C. To confirm seat count and accessibility signs.

D. To verify hazards are controlled using formal safety methods such as FMEA.

4. Why does CBTC testing continue after commissioning?

A. To monitor performance, detect issues early, and maintain system safety and reliability.

B. To qualify for advertising incentives.

C. To avoid retraining operators.

D. To guarantee long-distance travel certification.

Answer Key

1. B

2. C

3. D

4. A
