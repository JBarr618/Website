Module 7A: What is an AC Single Rail Track Circuit?

An AC single rail track circuit is a type of train detection system that utilizes alternating current and employs insulated rail joints on only one of the two running rails to define the boundaries of a track block. The other rail remains continuous, providing a common electrical path for multiple track circuits and often serving as the return for AC traction current in electrified areas. This configuration simplifies the track work and reduces the number of insulated joints required.

In this setup, the AC power source is connected between the insulated rail and the continuous rail at the beginning of a track block. Similarly, the AC-sensitive receiver is connected between the insulated rail and the continuous rail at the end of the same block. The alternating current signal flows from the source, along the insulated rail, through the train wheels and axles (when present), to the continuous rail, and back to the receiver.

When a train occupies the track section, its conductive wheels and axles create a low-impedance path (a shunt) between the insulated rail and the continuous rail. This shunt diverts the majority of the AC current away from the receiver, causing its output (e.g., relay de-energization) to change state, indicating that the track block is occupied. The "single rail" designation refers to the fact that only one of the two rails is segmented by insulated joints to create the defined electrical block for the track circuit.
