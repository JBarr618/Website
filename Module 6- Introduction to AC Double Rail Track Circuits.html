Module 6: Introduction to AC Double Rail Track Circuits

As railway systems evolved, particularly with the advent of electric traction, the limitations of DC track circuits in the presence of AC propulsion return currents became apparent. To overcome this interference, Alternating Current (AC) track circuits were developed. Like their DC counterparts, AC track circuits utilize the rails as conductors to detect the presence of trains within a defined block. However, instead of a direct current, they employ an alternating current signal, typically at a frequency that differs significantly from the AC traction power frequency (e.g., 50 or 60 Hz) to minimize interference.

In an AC double rail track circuit, both rails of the track are insulated from the adjacent track sections by insulated rail joints. An AC power source is connected to the rails at one end of the block, and a specialized AC-sensitive relay or receiver is connected at the other end. When the track section is unoccupied, the AC current flows from the source, through both rails, to the receiver, energizing it. The energized state of the relay indicates that the track section is clear.

When a train enters the block, its wheels and axles create a low-impedance path (a shunt) across both rails, similar to a DC track circuit. This shunt diverts the AC current away from the sensitive receiver, causing it to de-energize. The de-energized state signals that the track section is occupied. A key component in AC double rail track circuits, especially in electrified territory, is the impedance bond. These are inductive devices connected across the rails at the insulated joints. They allow the low-frequency AC traction return current to flow continuously along the rails while presenting a high impedance to the higher frequency AC signal of the track circuit, thus preventing interference.
