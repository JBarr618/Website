Module 9A: What is a Type C Track Circuit?

A Type C Track Circuit is an advanced form of AC track circuit engineered to provide reliable train detection and robust broken rail detection, particularly in challenging environments like electrified railways with significant AC traction return currents. The core principle of a Type C circuit involves the simultaneous transmission of two distinct alternating current frequencies along the rails of a track block.

One of these frequencies is primarily dedicated to train detection. When a train enters the track block, its wheels and axles create a low-impedance shunt across the rails, affecting the amplitude and/or phase of this train detection frequency as received at the other end of the block. The receiver is designed to detect these changes and indicate occupancy.

The second AC frequency is specifically used for broken rail detection. A break in either rail within the track block will significantly alter the impedance characteristics for this particular frequency. This change in impedance results in a detectable change in the received signal level or phase of the broken rail detection frequency. The receiver continuously monitors this frequency for such changes, providing an indication of a rail break even when a train is not present.

The receiver in a Type C track circuit is a sophisticated piece of equipment containing filters to separate the two transmitted frequencies and independent detection circuits to analyze each frequency for the specific changes associated with train occupancy and broken rails. This dual-frequency approach, combined with advanced filtering and detection techniques, provides a high degree of immunity to interference from AC traction currents and offers a more comprehensive approach to track integrity monitoring compared to simpler track circuit designs.
