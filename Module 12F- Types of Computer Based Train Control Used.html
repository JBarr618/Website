Module 12F: Types of Computer Based Train Control Used

Computer Based Train Control (CBTC) systems can be categorized in several ways, reflecting their architectural design, the level of automation they enable, and the communication technologies they employ. Understanding these classifications helps explain the flexibility of CBTC across diverse rail systems.

1. By Architecture

  • Centralized CBTC: A central control center receives data from all trains, calculates movement authorities and speed profiles, and sends commands back to each train. This setup relies heavily on continuous communication.
  • Distributed (Train-Centric) CBTC: More processing occurs on the train itself. Trains may share information directly with one another, reducing dependency on a single central processor. This approach improves scalability and resilience.

2. By Grade of Automation (GoA)

  • GoA 1 – Manual Operation: Driver handles all tasks; CBTC provides protection and situational data.
  • GoA 2 – Semi-Automatic Operation (STO): CBTC automates stopping/starting; driver handles doors and emergencies.
  • GoA 3 – Driverless Train Operation (DTO): No driver; attendant may be onboard. CBTC controls full movement and door operation.
  • GoA 4 – Unattended Train Operation (UTO): Fully automated with no onboard staff. Often paired with platform screen doors for safety.

3. By Communication Technology

  • Radio-Based: Wi-Fi, LTE, or licensed-band radios provide continuous train-to-control center communication.
  • Inductive Loops and Balises: Often used for positioning or periodic updates rather than primary data channels.
  • Public Cellular (LTE/5G): Emerging option for reducing dedicated infrastructure costs, but raises safety/security concerns.

These types can be combined. For example, a GoA 4 system may use centralized architecture and LTE communication. Railroads choose CBTC configurations based on traffic type, budget, automation goals, and local constraints.
