Types of Computer Based Train Control Used

Computer Based Train Control systems can be used in different ways depending on the railroad, transit system, level of automation, and communication technology.

CBTC is flexible because it can be designed for manual train operation, semi-automatic operation, driverless operation, or fully unattended operation.

1. Centralized CBTC

In a centralized CBTC system, most of the control processing is handled by central control equipment or wayside control equipment.

The central system receives train information, calculates Movement Authority, creates speed profiles, and sends commands back to each train.

2. Distributed CBTC

In a distributed CBTC system, more processing is handled by the onboard train equipment.

Trains may share information with wayside equipment or other trains. This can reduce dependence on one central processor and improve system flexibility.

3. GoA 1 - Manual Operation

In Grade of Automation 1, the train operator manually controls the train. CBTC may still provide train protection, speed supervision, and operating information.

4. GoA 2 - Semi-Automatic Train Operation

In Grade of Automation 2, the CBTC system can automatically control starting, stopping, and train speed.

A train operator is still onboard and normally handles doors, emergencies, and degraded operation.

5. GoA 3 - Driverless Train Operation

In Grade of Automation 3, the train operates without a driver in the cab. The system controls train movement and doors.

Staff may still be onboard to assist passengers or respond to emergency conditions.

6. GoA 4 - Unattended Train Operation

In Grade of Automation 4, train operation is fully automatic. No onboard train operator or attendant is required for normal operation.

GoA 4 systems are often used with additional safety features such as platform screen doors, intrusion detection, and strong system monitoring.

7. Radio-Based CBTC

Most modern CBTC systems use radio-based communication. This may include Wi-Fi, LTE, private radio networks, or licensed industrial radio systems.

Radio communication allows trains and control equipment to continuously exchange train location, speed, Movement Authority, and system status.

8. Balises and Inductive Loops

Some systems use balises, transponders, or inductive loops to support train positioning and data transfer at specific points along the track.

These devices are usually used to help confirm location instead of replacing the continuous communication network.

9. Public Cellular and Future Communication

Some newer systems may consider LTE, 5G, or other cellular-based communication methods. These can reduce infrastructure needs but must be carefully evaluated for safety, security, reliability, and coverage.

Review Questions

1. What is the main difference between centralized and distributed CBTC?

A. Centralized systems process control data centrally; distributed systems allow more local onboard processing.

B. Distributed systems require more wayside signals.

C. Centralized CBTC only works on single-track lines.

D. Distributed CBTC uses satellite GPS for all data transmission.

2. What describes a GoA 4 CBTC system?

A. Driver controls the train with ATO assistance.

B. Train operates automatically but a conductor is always onboard.

C. Automated start and stop with driver managing doors and safety.

D. Fully automated operation with no onboard staff required.

3. What communication technology is commonly used in CBTC?

A. Satellite dish communication.

B. Radio-based systems such as Wi-Fi, LTE, or private radio because continuous two-way communication is required.

C. Manual signaling by handheld radios only.

D. Acoustic sensors and intercom networks.

Answer Key

1. A

2. D

3. B
