🚦 Module 12C - Types of CBTC

What Types of Computer Based Train Control?

Computer Based Train Control systems can be grouped into different types based on system architecture, level of automation, and the communication technology used.

These categories help explain how CBTC systems are designed and how they support safe, efficient, and automated train operation.

1. Centralized CBTC

In a centralized CBTC system, the main control equipment is located in a central control center or wayside control location.

The central system receives train location and status information, calculates Movement Authority, creates speed profiles, and sends this information back to the train.

2. Distributed CBTC

In a distributed CBTC system, more processing and decision-making may be performed by onboard train equipment.

Trains may share information with wayside equipment or other trains to help maintain safe separation. This can reduce some dependence on one central control point.

3. Grade of Automation

CBTC systems can also be classified by Grade of Automation, often called GoA. The Grade of Automation describes how much of the train operation is performed automatically.

GoA 1 - Manual Operation

In GoA 1, the train operator controls starting, stopping, speed, and door operation. CBTC may still provide train protection and operating information.

GoA 2 - Semi-Automatic Train Operation

In GoA 2, the CBTC system may automatically control train starting, stopping, and speed. A train operator is still present and is usually responsible for door control, emergencies, and degraded operation.

GoA 3 - Driverless Train Operation

In GoA 3, the train operates without a driver in the cab. The system controls movement and doors, but staff may still be onboard to assist passengers or handle emergencies.

GoA 4 - Unattended Train Operation

In GoA 4, the system is fully automated. There is no driver or onboard operating staff required for normal train movement.

4. Communication-Based Types

Most modern CBTC systems use radio communication. These systems may use Wi-Fi, LTE, private radio networks, or other industrial wireless communication systems.

Reliable communication is critical because train location, speed, Movement Authority, and control commands must be exchanged continuously between the train and the CBTC system.

5. Balises and Reference Points

Some CBTC systems also use balises, transponders, or fixed reference points to help trains confirm their exact location along the track.

These devices do not usually provide continuous high-speed communication, but they help improve train positioning accuracy.
