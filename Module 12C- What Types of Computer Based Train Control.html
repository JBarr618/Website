Module 12C: What Types of Computer Based Train Control?

Computer Based Train Control (CBTC) systems can be categorized in several ways, reflecting their architectural design, the level of automation they enable, and the communication technologies they employ. Understanding these different classifications helps to appreciate the versatility and adaptability of CBTC in various railway environments.

1. Based on Architecture

  • Centralized CBTC: In this common architecture, a central control center serves as the primary processing hub. It receives train location and status information from all trains, calculates safe movement authorities and speed profiles, and transmits these commands back to the trains. This model relies heavily on a robust communication link between the trains and the central system.
  • Distributed CBTC (or Train-Centric CBTC): This emerging architecture shifts more of the processing power and decision-making capability to the trains themselves. Trains can potentially communicate directly with each other to maintain safe separation and coordinate movements, reducing the reliance on a central entity for some critical functions. This approach can offer benefits in terms of scalability and resilience.

2. Based on Grade of Automation (GoA)

CBTC is a key technology that enables different levels of automation in train operation, as defined by international standards (IEC 62290):

  • GoA 1 (Manual Operation): The driver is responsible for all aspects of train operation, including starting, stopping, speed control, and door operation, while adhering to signals. CBTC in this context primarily provides enhanced train protection and real-time information to the driver.
  • GoA 2 (Semi-Automatic Operation - STO): The CBTC system (specifically the Automatic Train Operation - ATO subsystem) automates starting and stopping accurately at stations. However, a driver typically retains responsibility for door operation, handling emergencies, and potentially manual driving in degraded modes.
  • GoA 3 (Driverless Train Operation - DTO): The CBTC system controls starting, stopping, and door operation automatically. There is no driver on board, but train attendants may be present to handle passenger information, emergencies, and manual operation if required.
  • GoA 4 (Unattended Train Operation - UTO): The CBTC system fully automates all operational aspects, including starting, stopping, door operation, and handling planned contingencies, without any on-train staff. Platform screen doors are often a prerequisite for safety in UTO systems.

3. Based on Communication Technology

The reliable and continuous exchange of data is fundamental to CBTC. Various wireless communication technologies are employed:

  • Radio-Based Systems: These are the most common, utilizing different frequency bands (e.g., Wi-Fi, cellular, proprietary industrial radio) and communication protocols. Coverage is typically provided through strategically placed radio base stations or leaky feeder cables along the track.
  • Inductive Loops and Balises: While not typically used for continuous high-bandwidth communication in full CBTC, inductive loops and balises can play a role in precise train positioning and transmitting discrete data points.

Note: These categories are not mutually exclusive, and a specific CBTC implementation might combine elements from different types. For example, a system could have a centralized architecture that supports Grade of Automation 4 using a radio-based communication network. The specific requirements of the railway (e.g., urban metro, mainline, freight) often dictate the most suitable type of CBTC system.
