Module 4: Introduction to Interlockings

Interlockings are crucial components of railroad signaling systems, designed to prevent conflicting train movements at complex track junctions and crossings. An interlocking is an arrangement of signals and track switches (also called points) that are interconnected in such a way that conflicting movements are physically impossible. The system ensures that only one train can occupy a specific route through the interlocking at any given time, eliminating the risk of collisions.

The core principle of an interlocking is route locking. When a train is authorized to proceed through an interlocking, the switches along its route are locked in the correct position, and conflicting routes are prevented from being set up. This is typically achieved through a combination of mechanical and electrical locking mechanisms. Signals are also controlled by the interlocking logic, displaying aspects that correspond to the established route and the status of the switches.

Interlockings can range in complexity from simple junctions with a few switches and signals to large, intricate layouts with numerous tracks, switches, and signals. The design and operation of an interlocking must adhere to strict safety standards and regulations to guarantee the safe and efficient movement of trains. They are essential for managing traffic flow and preventing accidents in areas where train routes converge or intersect.
