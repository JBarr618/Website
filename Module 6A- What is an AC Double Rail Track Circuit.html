Module 6A: What is an AC Double Rail Track Circuit?

An AC double rail track circuit is a type of train detection system that utilizes alternating current and has both rails of the track electrically isolated from adjacent sections by insulated rail joints. This means that a defined segment of track, the "block," has a clear electrical boundary on both running rails. An AC power source is connected across these two insulated rails at one end of the block, and an AC-sensitive detection device, typically a relay or a more sophisticated electronic receiver, is connected across the rails at the opposite end.

The fundamental principle of operation relies on the impedance of the circuit. When the track section is clear of any trains, the AC signal flows unimpeded from the source, through both rails, to the receiver, which is designed to be sensitive to the specific frequency of the applied AC. This energizes the receiver, indicating an unoccupied track.

The presence of a train within the block changes the electrical characteristics of the circuit. The metal wheels and axles of the train create a low-impedance shunt across the two rails. This low-impedance path offers an easier route for the AC current to flow, effectively bypassing the high impedance of the receiver at the other end. Consequently, the current through the receiver drops significantly, causing it to de-energize and signal that the track section is now occupied. The "double rail" aspect signifies that both rails are part of the signaling circuit and are insulated at the block boundaries.
