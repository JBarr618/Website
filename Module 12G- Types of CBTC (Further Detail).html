Module 12G: Types of Computer Based Train Control Used (Further Detail)

To further clarify the types of Computer Based Train Control (CBTC) systems in operation and development, we can look at specific examples and nuances within the main categories:

1. By Architecture – Real-World Examples and Nuances

  • Centralized CBTC Dominance: Most current CBTC systems use a centralized architecture. Examples include:
    • Siemens Trainguard MT – deployed in many metros globally
    • Thales SelTrac – widely used in urban transit
    • Bombardier CITYFLO (now Alstom) – installed in several cities worldwide
  • Hybrid Evolution: Newer CBTC systems are exploring hybrid models, combining centralized safety management with distributed train-based logic for resilience and scalability.

2. By Grade of Automation (GoA) – Deployment Trends

  • GoA 2 as Entry Point: Many new systems start with Semi-Automatic Operation (STO) to improve capacity while maintaining a human operator for doors and emergencies.
  • GoA 3/4 Growth: There is growing adoption of Driverless (DTO) and Unattended Train Operation (UTO) on new metro lines in cities like:
    • Paris (Line 14)
    • Singapore (North East Line)
    • Dubai (Red and Green Lines)
  • Mainline Challenges: Full GoA 3/4 automation on mixed-traffic mainlines is more difficult due to longer braking distances, complex routing, and obstacle detection requirements. These often operate at GoA 2 or integrate with ETCS Level 3.

3. By Communication Technology – Specific Systems

  • Radio Spectrum: Systems use licensed bands, Wi-Fi, or proprietary RF systems depending on interference, bandwidth, and regulatory considerations.
  • Millimeter Wave: Experimental systems are exploring ultra-high frequency mmWave to improve bandwidth for advanced CBTC services.
  • Public Cellular Networks: Some CBTC systems are testing LTE or 5G for cost reduction. While promising, public networks raise concerns about latency, reliability, and cybersecurity in safety-critical environments.

In short, CBTC systems are tailored to the infrastructure and operational needs of the railway. Technology choices balance cost, reliability, automation goals, and safety integrity levels.
