Module 7: Introduction to AC Single Rail Track Circuits

In contrast to AC double rail track circuits, AC single rail track circuits utilize insulated rail joints on only one of the two running rails to define the track block. The other rail remains electrically continuous throughout multiple track circuit blocks and is typically used as the return path for AC traction power in electrified territory, as well as the return path for the AC track circuit current.

In a typical AC single rail track circuit setup, the AC power source is connected between the insulated rail and the continuous rail at one end of the block. The AC-sensitive receiver is connected similarly at the other end. The AC signal flows from the source, through the insulated rail, across the receiver, and returns via the continuous rail. When a train enters the block, its wheels and axles shunt the insulated rail to the continuous rail, providing a low-impedance path that bypasses the receiver, causing it to de-energize and indicate occupancy.

A key advantage of AC single rail track circuits is that they require fewer insulated rail joints compared to double rail circuits, potentially reducing installation and maintenance costs. However, they have the disadvantage of only being able to detect breaks in the insulated rail. A break in the continuous rail might not be detected by the track circuit. Therefore, their application is often limited to specific situations and may require additional safety measures.
